**1. Understanding the Core Principle: Contrast and Hierarchy**  
Effective font pairing relies on creating clear visual contrast while maintaining harmony. The golden rule is to use one **display font** (for headlines) and one **text font** (for body copy). Display fonts should be bold, decorative, or unique (e.g., a slab serif like *Playfair Display*), while text fonts need high readability at small sizes (e.g., a clean sans-serif like *Source Sans Pro*). To start, pick a primary font for your headlines—choose one with strong personality (e.g., a geometric sans-serif like *Montserrat*). Then, pair it with a neutral, highly legible body font (e.g., *Merriweather* or *Roboto*). Use tools like **Google Fonts** (filter by categories like “Serif” vs. “Sans Serif”) or **Fontjoy** (an AI-powered generator that suggests pairs based on contrast). Always test your pair at actual text sizes: headlines at 30–48px, body at 14–18px.

**2. Actionable Steps to Implement Your Pair**  
First, **limit to two fonts** (maximum three if you need a third for captions or buttons). Use a tool like **Typewolf** to see real-world examples of your chosen pair in action. Second, **test contrast in size and weight**: pair a heavy, uppercase display font (e.g., *Oswald* for headlines) with a light, lowercase body font (e.g., *Lora* for paragraphs). Third, **avoid similar proportions**—never pair two serifs or two sans-serifs with the same x-height or stroke weight (e.g., avoid pairing *Times New Roman* with *Georgia*; they are too similar).
